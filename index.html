If something happened

How reporting works

Five steps, and you decide how far to go at each one.

01

Notice & note it down

Jot down what happened, when, and where, if you're comfortable. It's optional, but it helps whoever follows up.

02

Choose how to reach out

Use the anonymous QR form, drop a note at a BRIDGE station, or approach an office directly. All routes lead to the same support.

03

Pick the right office

Discipline Unit for conduct and policy concerns, Guidance & Counseling for support and referrals. Not sure? Either office can point you the right way.

04

A confidential follow-up

A staff member reaches out on your terms — no public disclosure, no pressure to name names before you're ready.

05

Ongoing support

Referrals, check-ins, or simply knowing who to talk to next time. This hub stays open long after a report is filed.

Student facilitators are not counselors or legal experts — sensitive concerns are always referred to the proper office.

Common terms

Words worth knowing

Plain-language definitions, not clinical ones — a starting point for conversations, not the last word.

Gender Identity
A person's internal sense of their own gender, which may or may not match the sex they were assigned at birth.
Gender Expression
How someone presents their gender outwardly — through clothing, behavior, voice, or appearance.
Sexual Orientation
Who a person is emotionally or physically attracted to. Separate from gender identity.
LGBTQIA+
An umbrella term covering lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer, intersex, asexual, and other identities beyond the label.
Ally
Someone who supports LGBTQIA+ and gender-diverse people without needing to share their identity.
Misgendering
Referring to someone using words, pronouns, or terms that don't match their gender identity — usually unintentional, but worth correcting.
Stereotype
A fixed, oversimplified assumption about a group of people, often based on gender, that ignores individual differences.
Minority Stress
The extra, ongoing stress that comes from navigating a world that isn't always built for your identity.
Ask without fear

Frequently asked questions

Real questions submitted anonymously during BRIDGE events.

What's a respectful way to ask about someone's pronouns?

Introduce your own first — "Hi, I'm Alex, I use he/him. What about you?" It normalizes the question and takes the pressure off. If someone would rather not share, that's okay too — just ask what they'd prefer to be called.

A friend makes an offensive joke. What do I do?

You don't have to make it a confrontation. A quiet "hey, that one didn't land for me" in the moment is usually enough. If it keeps happening, a private conversation afterward tends to go further than calling someone out in front of others.

Is it wrong to make a mistake if I'm still learning?

No. Nobody expects perfect language on day one. What matters is how you respond when you're corrected — a quick "thanks, got it" and moving on beats over-apologizing or getting defensive.

How can I support someone without speaking over them?

Ask what kind of support they actually want before jumping in — sometimes it's advice, sometimes it's just someone to listen. Let them lead the conversation about their own experience.

Where can a student report harassment?

Start with the FEU Tech Discipline Unit for policy violations, or the Guidance and Counseling Office if you'd rather talk it through first. Both routes are outlined in the Reporting section above, and neither requires your name if you're not ready to share it.
